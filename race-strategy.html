Fueling · The Plan
The Rule

At half marathon pace, you burn through glycogen in roughly 75–90 minutes. You will hit the wall before you feel depleted — by the time you feel it, it's too late. Fuel on a schedule, not on hunger. This is the most common first-race mistake at this level.

Night Before
Carbohydrate load. Simple, familiar foods. Pasta, rice, bread, potatoes. No heavy sauces, no new foods, nothing high-fiber. 600–800 calories of carbs with dinner. Drink water consistently through the evening — not a flood right before bed, just steady intake.
Race Morning
400–600 cal, 2.5–3 hrs before gun. Nothing new. Oats with banana, toast with peanut butter, or whatever you've eaten before a long run. White rice works well. Avoid eggs, dairy, high-fat foods — they slow gastric emptying. Coffee is fine if it's your routine; if not, today is not the day to start.
30 Min Out
Gel #1 + 4–6 oz water. The pre-load. Take one gel 25–30 minutes before the gun. This tops off blood glucose and reduces how aggressively you'll need to fuel during the race. Chase it with water, not a sports drink. Don't take this right at the start line — timing matters.
Mile 4–5
Gel #2. First in-race fuel. Don't wait until you feel like you need it. Mile 4–5 is roughly 25–30 minutes in — this is the right window. Take it with water at the next aid station. Chew gels if that's easier; don't try to swallow on a hard breath. Slow slightly if you need to — it's worth it.
Mile 8–9
Gel #3. The critical one. This is the most important gel of the race. Miles 9–11 are where glycogen depletion hits. Taking this at mile 8 puts fuel in your bloodstream exactly when you need it. If you skip this one, miles 10–13 will feel significantly harder than they should. Don't skip it.
Mile 11–12
Optional gel #4 or chew. If available. At this point you're 15–20 minutes from the finish. A gel here won't fully digest in time, but the simple sugars hit fast enough to help. If you have one and feel yourself fading, take it. If you feel strong, skip it — don't risk GI issues in the final miles.
First Race Note · Read This Gels can cause GI distress if you haven't practiced with them in training. Use the same brand and flavor you've trained with. If you haven't trained with gels, use Gatorade at every aid station instead — it's slower acting but easier on the stomach. Never try a new product on race day.
Hydration · The Plan
Key Biscayne in April means heat and humidity. Do not underestimate this. Dehydration at this pace accelerates perceived effort and degrades form. The goal is to arrive at the start line already hydrated — not to drink aggressively during the race.
2 Days Out
Consistent water intake. ~3L/day. Your urine should be pale yellow by Thursday. If it's dark, you're behind. Don't chug — just drink consistently through the day.
Race Morning
16–20 oz water with breakfast. Sip until 30 min before. Stop drinking large amounts 30 minutes before the gun to avoid sloshing. If you're thirsty right before the start, a few small sips are fine.
During Race
4–6 oz at every aid station. Every one. Don't skip aid stations even if you feel fine. In the heat, thirst lags behind actual need by 10–15 minutes. Alternate water and electrolyte drink if available. Slow to a near-walk at stations if you need to drink properly; the time cost is worth it.
Electrolytes
Salt tabs or electrolyte caps — 1 per hour. In humid Miami heat you'll sweat significantly. Plain water alone can dilute sodium and cause hyponatremia in longer efforts. If you have electrolyte capsules (Precision Hydration, SaltStick, etc.), take one around miles 4 and 9 with water. If not, make sure you're using the sports drink at aid stations, not just water.
Warmup Protocol · Race Morning
T–40 min
Easy jog. 8–10 minutes, very relaxed. Not a workout. This raises core temp, gets blood moving to the legs, and signals to your nervous system that something is happening. If you skip the warmup, mile 1 of the race becomes your warmup — at race pace. That's too expensive.
T–30 min
Dynamic drills. 5 minutes. Leg swings (front/back, side to side), hip circles, high knees, butt kicks, A-skips. Not stretching — movement. Static stretching before a race reduces power output. Save it for after.
T–20 min
4–6 strides at goal pace. Full recovery between each. Each stride is 20–25 seconds at your target race pace — not sprinting, not jogging. Full stop between each. This primes the neuromuscular system so goal pace doesn't feel like a shock at the gun. After your last stride, goal pace should feel almost familiar.
T–10 min
Corral. Pre-race gel. Calm. Take your pre-race gel now. Find your corral position — seed toward the front of your wave, not the back. Get away from people who are visibly anxious. Their energy is contagious and you don't need it. Breathe slowly. You've done the work.

Post-Race · The First Hour
0–15 min
Keep moving. Walk, don't sit. Stopping cold after a hard effort pools blood in the legs. Walk for 10 minutes after you cross. Your heart rate will thank you and the recovery starts faster.
15 min
Carbs + protein within 30 minutes of finishing. The glycogen window is real. Banana + chocolate milk, a recovery shake, a bagel with peanut butter — something immediately. Your muscles are most receptive to glycogen resynthesis in the 30 minutes post-race. Don't wait until you feel hungry.
1 Hour
Real meal. Hydrate with electrolytes. A full meal within an hour. Protein, carbs, some fat. Don't celebrate with alcohol immediately — it disrupts muscle protein synthesis and dehydrates you further. One celebratory drink with food is fine. Two before a meal is a recovery mistake.
